📄 Paper Deep Dive #1: Mechanical Enforcement for LLM Governance

Title: Mechanical Enforcement for LLM Governance: Evidence of Governance-Task Decoupling in Financial Decision Systems

Authors: José Manuel de la Chica Rodríguez, Carlos Martí-González (Santander AI Lab)

Date: May 2026

Link: [arXiv:2605.14744](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.14744v1)

The Core Problem

When an LLM both interprets a compliance policy and decides whether it has satisfied that policy, you have a principal-agent failure. The model can produce deferrals that look compliant ("further review is needed due to the complexity of the situation") while carrying zero decision-relevant information.

The authors tested this on a synthetic banking domain with five governance flags (AML, KYC, SANCTIONS, INSIDER, CONCENTRATION) across 2,400 cases. Their finding: 27% of deferrals under text-only governance are vacuous — they cite no specific case parameters, flags, or thresholds.

The Fix: Four Mechanical Primitives

Rather than trusting the LLM to self-govern, they propose R2 (Mechanical Enforcement) — four primitives operating outside the model's interpretive loop:

Primitive	What It Does	Blocks	
Hard Gates	Pre-LLM decision boundaries on risk, completeness, and regulatory flag thresholds	Empty deferrals on clear-cut cases	
I6Q	Enforces minimum argument length (≥10 tokens) and lexical diversity (TTR ≥0.4)	Cosmetic explanations	
CEFL	Externalizes candidate generation before scoring	Selection-by-negation bias	
E3	Commit-reveal protocol: random seed is committed before scoring begins	Seed-conditioning attacks	

The Results

Metric	Text-Only (R1)	Mechanical (R2)	Change	
Cosmetic Deadlock Rate (CDL)	27.3%	7.4%	−73%	
Deferral Information Utilization (DIU)	0.298	0.766	+157%	
Task Accuracy (MCC)	0.433	0.884	+104%	

The critical finding is governance-task decoupling: under structural stress (information loss), R2 achieves its best governance (CDL = 0.088, DIU = 0.852) and its worst task accuracy (MCC = 0.285) simultaneously. Text-only governance degrades on both axes at once.

For our team: If your compliance AI uses natural-language policy prompts with no external enforcement, you're measuring task accuracy but not governance quality. The two diverge under stress. Add CDL and DIU to your evaluation pipeline.

📄 Paper Deep Dive #2: LLM Output Drift

Title: LLM Output Drift: Cross-Provider Validation & Mitigation for Financial Workflows

Authors: Raffi Khatchadourian, Rolando Franco (IBM Financial Services Market)

Date: November 2025

Link: [arXiv:2511.07585](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2511.07585v1)

The Core Problem

LLMs in financial workflows produce nondeterministic outputs even at temperature=0. This isn't theoretical — Anthropic's September 2025 incident showed random anomalies from a miscompiled sampling algorithm. For compliance, this is fatal: if you can't reproduce an AI-generated decision, you can't audit it.

The Shocking Finding

The authors tested five models (7B–120B parameters) across 480 runs and found a stark inverse relationship between model size and determinism:

Model	Parameters	Consistency at T=0.0	
Qwen2.5-7B	7B	100%	
Granite-3-8B	8B	100%	
Llama-3.3-70B	70B	75%	
Mistral-Medium-2505	50B	56%	
GPT-OSS-120B	120B	12.5%	

A 120B-parameter model fails determinism 87.5% of the time regardless of configuration. This challenges the assumption that bigger is always better for production.

The Three-Tier Framework

Tier	Models	Use Case	
Tier 1	Granite-3-8B, Qwen2.5-7B	Audit-ready deterministic workflows (reconciliations, regulatory reporting)	
Tier 2	Llama-3.3-70B, Mistral-Medium	High-capability tasks with tolerance for minor variance	
Tier 3	GPT-OSS-120B	Research/exploration only — not suitable for compliance	

Task-Specific Sensitivity

- SQL generation: Remains 100% consistent even at T=0.2 (structured output)
- RAG tasks: Drift rises to 25–75% at T=0.2 (creative synthesis)
- JSON summarization: Intermediate sensitivity

The authors also validated cross-provider consistency — local (Ollama) and cloud (IBM watsonx.ai) deployments produce identical outputs for Tier 1 models, enabling hybrid strategies.

For our team: If your compliance AI uses a frontier model for structured tasks (SQL, JSON), you're overpaying and under-determining. Tier 1 small models are now the rational choice for audit-critical workflows.

📄 Paper Deep Dive #3: Banking Done Right

Title: Banking Done Right: Redefining Retail Banking with Language-Centric AI

Authors: Xin Jie Chua et al. (Universiti Malaya, YTL AI Labs, Ryt Bank)

Date: October 2025

Link: [arXiv:2510.07645](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2510.07645v1)

The Core Achievement

Ryt AI is the first globally regulator-approved deployment where conversational AI functions as the primary banking interface — not just advisory. Users say "Transfer RM10 to Mike" and the system executes the full transaction pipeline.

The Architecture

Four specialized agents, each with a task-specific LoRA adapter on a shared in-house LLM (ILMU, <10B parameters):

Agent	Role	LoRA Adapter	
Guardrails	Blocks jailbreaks, adversarial prompts, unsupported requests	Guardrail-tuned	
Intent Classifier	Routes user input to appropriate action agent	Intent-tuned	
Payment Agent	Extracts fields, validates, executes transfers	Payment-tuned	
FAQ Agent	RAG-based answers from curated knowledge base	FAQ-tuned	

The Compliance Design

Three defense-in-depth mechanisms:

1. Deterministic Guardrails: Structured JSON output (`isSafe`, `guardrailViolation`, `message`) — not free-text filtering
2. Human-in-the-Loop: All fund transfers require explicit user confirmation (Approve/Decline/Edit) before execution
3. Stateless Memory Architecture: All session data is discarded after the conversation ends — no persistent storage of prompts, inferences, or extracted fields

The system is fully in-house: ILMU is developed internally, hosted on bank infrastructure, with verifiable control over training data and inference processes. This wasn't a design preference — it was a regulatory requirement from Bank Negara Malaysia.

Performance

Tested against GPT-4o, GPT-4o mini, Gemini 2.0 Flash, and Gemini 2.0 Flash-Lite on 2,000 conversational cases. The in-house ILMU with LoRA adapters matched or exceeded commercial models on intent classification accuracy while maintaining deterministic behavior.

For our team: This is the blueprint for regulator-approved AI-native banking. The key insight: sovereignty (in-house model, on-premise hosting, stateless architecture) isn't overhead — it's the price of approval.

📄 Paper Deep Dive #4: The Limits of AI in Financial Services

Title: The Limits of AI in Financial Services

Authors: Isabella Loaiza, Roberto Rigobon (MIT Sloan)

Date: March 2025

Link: [arXiv:2503.22035](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2503.22035v1)

The Core Argument

AI is a universal approximator — it excels at data-driven prediction but fails at small datasets, subjective probabilities, and contexts requiring human judgment, relationships, and ethics. The authors argue that the financial sector's rush to automate everything is overlooking fundamental boundaries.

The EPOCH Framework

Five irreplaceable human capabilities that AI cannot replicate:

Capability	What It Means in Finance	Why AI Fails	
Empathy	Understanding client distress during market crashes, job loss, or bereavement	No emotional grounding; can simulate empathy but not feel it	
Presence	Being physically available for high-stakes negotiations, trust-building	No situational awareness; misses body language, tone, context	
Opinion	Forming subjective judgments under radical uncertainty (e.g., "is this client trustworthy?")	Cannot handle ambiguity without training data; overfits to historical patterns	
Creativity	Designing novel financial products, structuring bespoke deals	Combines existing patterns but doesn't originate; bounded by training distribution	
Hope	Inspiring confidence during crises, maintaining long-term client relationships	No stake in outcomes; cannot genuinely believe in a client's future	

The ATM Parallel

The authors draw a direct analogy to ATMs: when automated teller machines proliferated, bank teller jobs didn't disappear — they evolved. Tellers shifted from cash handling to sales, relationship management, and complex problem-solving. The total number of teller jobs actually increased for two decades post-ATM.

The same will happen with AI in finance: jobs won't be eliminated, they'll be redefined. The challenge is ensuring professionals adapt — leveraging AI's strengths (data processing, pattern detection, routine automation) while preserving human capabilities for trust, inclusion, innovation, and consumer experience.

Where AI Works vs. Where Humans Win

AI-Appropriate	Human-Required	
Credit scoring with large, structured datasets	Subjective credit assessment for thin-file borrowers	
Fraud detection on transaction patterns	Investigating novel fraud typologies with no historical precedent	
Regulatory document summarization	Interpreting regulatory intent during ambiguous enforcement	
Portfolio optimization with clear objectives	Bespoke wealth advice during life transitions	
Market-making and liquidity provision	Negotiating distressed debt restructuring	

The Governance Implication

The authors warn that over-reliance on AI for tasks requiring EPOCH capabilities creates systemic fragility: models trained on historical data fail precisely when the future diverges from the past (black swans, regime changes, novel crises). The 2008 financial crisis was partly a failure of quantitative models that assumed stable correlations.

For our team: This is the antidote to AI hype. Use it to frame your governance framework: which decisions can be fully automated, which require human-in-the-loop, and which should remain human-only? If you can't map every AI decision to one of these three buckets, your risk framework has gaps.
